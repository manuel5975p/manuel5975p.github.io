# Findings — Batch 10 (Mushrooms 91–100)

## Gold Tops (Psilocybe cubensis "Golden Teacher")

- **Scientific name:** *Psilocybe cubensis* (strain "Golden Teacher")
- **Habitat:** Native to subtropical regions; thrives on cattle dung and well-manured pastures in humid climates (Gulf Coast US, Mexico, Central/South America, Southeast Asia, Australia). The Golden Teacher is a cultivated strain typically grown indoors on grain and manure-based substrates.
- **Appearance:** Medium to large mushroom with a golden-yellow to caramel cap (3–8 cm), often with a slight central bump (umbo); gills are dark purplish-brown to black at maturity; stem is whitish, bruising blue when handled — a hallmark of psilocybin-containing species.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Psychoactive and controlled in most jurisdictions. Contains psilocybin and psilocin; not lethally toxic at typical doses but legally restricted and capable of producing intense psychological effects.
- **Interesting fact:** "Golden Teacher" emerged as a named strain in the 1980s and is famous among cultivators for its forgiving growth characteristics and reputedly "philosophical" experiential profile, making it the most popular teaching strain worldwide.

## Smart Contract Fungus (Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi)

- **Scientific name:** *Rhizophagus irregularis* (formerly *Glomus intraradices*), phylum Glomeromycota
- **Habitat:** Ubiquitous in soils worldwide, forming obligate symbioses with roots of ~80% of land plant species, including most crops, grasses, and trees.
- **Appearance:** Microscopic; produces no visible fruiting body. Identifiable by intraradical structures: tree-like arbuscules and oval vesicles within root cortical cells, plus large (40–800 μm) asexual spores in the soil.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Non-pathogenic and non-edible (microscopic); strictly mutualistic — exchanges soil-derived phosphorus, nitrogen, and water for plant-fixed carbon.
- **Interesting fact:** AMF networks act like biological "smart contracts": fungi preferentially deliver more phosphorus to plant partners that supply more carbon, and plants reciprocally reward generous fungal partners — a market-like reciprocal trade documented by Kiers et al. (2011) using isotope tracing. These common mycorrhizal networks (the "Wood Wide Web") can connect multiple plants, mediating multi-party resource exchange.

## Cryptococcus neoformans

- **Scientific name:** *Cryptococcus neoformans* (Basidiomycota, Tremellomycetes)
- **Habitat:** Globally distributed; particularly associated with bird droppings (especially pigeon guano), decaying wood, and soil enriched with avian excreta. Found in urban and rural environments worldwide.
- **Appearance:** A unicellular encapsulated yeast, 4–10 μm in diameter, with a thick polysaccharide capsule visible as a clear halo under India ink staining. No macroscopic fruiting body; produces hyphae only during sexual reproduction.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Pathogenic — a major opportunistic human pathogen. Inhalation of desiccated yeast cells or basidiospores can cause cryptococcosis: pulmonary infection that may disseminate to the central nervous system, causing life-threatening cryptococcal meningitis, especially in immunocompromised patients (HIV/AIDS, transplant recipients).
- **Interesting fact:** Cryptococcal meningitis kills an estimated 100,000+ people annually, predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa among HIV-positive individuals. The fungus's polysaccharide capsule is its key virulence factor, helping it evade phagocytosis — and birds are unaffected carriers because their high body temperature inhibits fungal growth.

## Lost Agaric (Pleurotus nebrodensis / historical Agarics)

- **Scientific name:** *Pleurotus nebrodensis* — broadly representative of "lost agarics"; many 18th–19th century described agarics are no longer recognized or located in the wild.
- **Habitat:** Originally described from Sicily's Madonie mountains on calcareous soils with *Cachrys ferulacea* roots; many similar historical taxa known only from European meadows or from herbarium specimens and color plates by Bulliard, Persoon, or Fries.
- **Appearance:** Typical agaric morphology: fleshy white-to-cream cap, decurrent gills, stout central-to-eccentric stipe; specific identifying features for many historical taxa are known only from period illustrations and brief Latin diagnoses, often impossible to match to modern collections.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Effectively extinct or "lost" in the wild for many such taxa; *P. nebrodensis* itself is edible but conservation-protected — harvesting is illegal.
- **Interesting fact:** *Pleurotus nebrodensis*, described by Italian mycologist Giuseppe Inzenga in 1863, was once thought extinct but rediscovered in the 1990s — it is now listed on the IUCN Red List as Critically Endangered, making it one of the only fungi formally protected by international conservation law.

## Death Cap, North American Invasion (Amanita phalloides)

- **Scientific name:** *Amanita phalloides*
- **Habitat:** Native to Europe but invasive across North America since the early 20th century, likely introduced with imported European hardwood seedlings (cork oak, chestnut). Now established with native and non-native oaks (*Quercus*), and notably spreading on the US West Coast (California, Oregon, Washington) and East Coast; recently jumped onto native oaks and pines.
- **Appearance:** Cap 5–15 cm, smooth, greenish-yellow to olive-bronze, occasionally pale; white free gills; white stipe with a skirt-like ring (annulus) and a prominent sac-like volva at the base; spore print white. Sweet honey-like odor when fresh.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Deadly toxic. Contains amatoxins (α-amanitin) which inhibit RNA polymerase II, causing fatal hepatic and renal failure 2–6 days after ingestion. Responsible for ~90% of mushroom poisoning fatalities worldwide.
- **Interesting fact:** Genetic studies (Pringle et al.) revealed *A. phalloides* in California reproduces unisexually — a single haploid genotype can mate with itself, allowing rapid clonal spread across new continents. A 2023 study identified indocyanine green as a potential antidote that blocks amatoxin uptake by hepatocytes.

## Dragon's Blood Mushroom (Hydnellum peckii)

- **Scientific name:** *Hydnellum peckii* (also known as "bleeding tooth fungus," "strawberries and cream")
- **Habitat:** Mycorrhizal partner of conifers (especially pines and spruces) in mossy coniferous forests across North America, Europe, Iran, and Korea; prefers acidic, nutrient-poor soils.
- **Appearance:** Young specimens are unmistakable: a velvety white to pinkish cap that exudes droplets of vivid red fluid resembling blood, secreted via guttation. Underside bears tooth-like spines (not gills) that produce spores. Cap (4–10 cm) becomes brown and irregular with age; flesh is corky and tough.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Inedible — not toxic but extremely bitter, peppery, and woody-tough, making consumption impractical. Not associated with poisoning.
- **Interesting fact:** The red exudate contains atromentin, a pigment with anticoagulant properties comparable to heparin; researchers have investigated it as a potential antithrombotic agent. The fungus also accumulates cesium-137 from soil, making it a useful bioindicator of nuclear fallout.

## Fairy Ring Mushroom (Marasmius oreades)

- **Scientific name:** *Marasmius oreades* (the "Scotch bonnet")
- **Habitat:** Lawns, meadows, pastures, golf courses, and grassy roadsides throughout temperate North America, Europe, and Asia; particularly common in summer and autumn after rain.
- **Appearance:** Small mushroom with a tan-to-buff cap (2–5 cm), bell-shaped when young, becoming flat with a low central umbo; pale, widely spaced gills; tough, wiry, fibrous stem that twists rather than snaps. Fruits in conspicuous arcs or complete rings on lawns.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Edible and prized — sweet, nutty flavor; commonly dried for soups and sauces. CAUTION: easily confused with the deadly *Clitocybe rivulosa* (sweat-causing *Clitocybe*) which grows in similar grassy habitats. *M. oreades* also has remarkable desiccation tolerance — dried specimens revive when rewetted.
- **Interesting fact:** Fairy rings form because the mycelium grows outward radially from a central origin, depleting nutrients in older inner soil while the active outer ring stimulates lush grass growth. European folklore variously attributed rings to dancing fairies, witches' covens, or dragon footprints; entering a ring at night was said to bring misfortune or supernatural abduction.

## Goblin's Gold (Schistostega pennata)

- **Scientific name:** *Schistostega pennata* (luminous moss). Note: technically a bryophyte rather than a true fungus, but commonly grouped with cave organisms in mushroom folklore.
- **Habitat:** Dimly lit caves, rock crevices, badger setts, hollow tree bases, and mine shafts across the Northern Hemisphere (Europe, North America, Japan); requires very low light and high humidity.
- **Appearance:** Filamentous protonema (juvenile growth) carpets cave floors with a striking golden-green glow; lens-shaped cells act as parabolic reflectors, focusing dim light onto chloroplasts and reflecting unused green wavelengths back — producing the eerie luminescent appearance. Mature gametophytes are tiny, feathery green plants.
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Non-edible (a moss); harmless and non-pathogenic; protected in many regions due to habitat sensitivity.
- **Interesting fact:** The "gold" is purely optical — the moss does not bioluminesce. German folklore held that the glow marked goblins' or dwarves' hidden treasure; folklore-seekers digging at glowing spots found only worthless dirt, hence "goblin's gold" became a metaphor for fool's treasure. (The bioluminescent counterpart in mushroom mythology is *Mycena lux-coeli*, a true luminous fungus from Japan.)

## Witch's Hat (Hygrocybe conica)

- **Scientific name:** *Hygrocybe conica* (the conical waxcap, "witch's hat")
- **Habitat:** Unimproved grasslands, mossy lawns, dunes, churchyards, and old pastures across Europe, North America, Asia, and Australasia; prefers nutrient-poor, undisturbed turf — making it an indicator of high-quality grassland habitat.
- **Appearance:** Sharply conical to pointed cap (2–5 cm) in vivid orange, scarlet, or yellow; waxy texture; pale yellow gills; slender fibrous stem. Diagnostic feature: the entire mushroom blackens dramatically when bruised or aged, often turning entirely jet-black — hence "witch's hat."
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Considered toxic / inedible. Some references list it as suspect; documented cases of gastrointestinal upset and at least one report of severe poisoning in China. Best avoided.
- **Interesting fact:** Waxcaps in the genus *Hygrocybe* were long assumed to be saprotrophic (decay-feeding), but stable isotope studies in the 2000s revealed they are likely biotrophic, possibly forming undescribed associations with mosses or grass roots — a major unsolved mystery in fungal ecology. Their presence is used in the UK's "CHEG" index to grade grassland conservation value.

## Zombie-Ant Fungus (Ophiocordyceps unilateralis)

- **Scientific name:** *Ophiocordyceps unilateralis* (a species complex with many host-specific cryptic species)
- **Habitat:** Tropical rainforests worldwide, especially the Amazon, Central African, and Southeast Asian rainforests; obligate parasite of carpenter ants (genus *Camponotus*) and related Formicidae.
- **Appearance:** Manifests as a slender, dark, club-shaped stroma (fruiting body) 1–5 cm long, growing out of the head or thorax of a dead ant clamped to vegetation. The fertile orange-to-brown perithecial swelling near the tip releases ascospores. The infected ant itself is the most diagnostic "feature."
- **Edibility / pathogenicity:** Pathogenic to insects (entomopathogenic); not directly pathogenic to humans and not consumed. Some related *Ophiocordyceps* and *Cordyceps* species are used in traditional Chinese medicine, but *O. unilateralis* is not.
- **Interesting fact:** The fungus manipulates host behavior with extraordinary precision: infected ants leave their colonies, climb to a specific height (~25 cm) on the underside of a leaf, orient toward the sun, and clamp their mandibles into a leaf vein in a "death grip" before dying — optimizing spore dispersal onto foraging trails below. Remarkably, recent research (Hughes et al., 2017) found the fungus does not invade the brain — it surrounds and controls muscle fibers directly, raising deep questions about how it coordinates such complex behavior.
