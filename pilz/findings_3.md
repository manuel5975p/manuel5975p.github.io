# Findings — Batch 3 (Mushrooms 21–30)

## Shaggy Ink Cap (Coprinus comatus)

- **Scientific name:** *Coprinus comatus*
- **Habitat:** Common across temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere; thrives in disturbed soil such as lawns, roadsides, gravel paths, meadows, and compacted ground. Often appears in groups after rain, late summer through autumn.
- **Appearance / ID:** Tall, cylindrical to bullet-shaped white cap (5–15 cm) covered in coarse, upturned shaggy scales — hence the nickname "Lawyer's Wig." Stem is white, hollow, with a movable ring. Gills start white, then turn pink, then black as the cap rapidly deliquesces (auto-digests) into an inky black liquid.
- **Edibility:** **Edible and choice** — but only when very young and still pure white inside. Must be consumed within hours of picking; the mushroom liquefies quickly. (Mild interaction warnings with alcohol exist, less severe than its relative *Coprinopsis atramentaria*.)
- **Interesting fact:** The black ink produced as the mushroom self-digests was historically used as actual writing ink, sometimes mixed with cloves; documents written in it can be authenticated by spores still embedded in the strokes.

## Autumn Skullcap (Galerina marginata)

- **Scientific name:** *Galerina marginata* (formerly *G. autumnalis*)
- **Habitat:** Worldwide on decaying hardwood and conifer logs, stumps, and buried wood. Common in forests of North America, Europe, and Asia, fruiting from late summer through late autumn (sometimes into winter).
- **Appearance / ID:** Small brownish mushroom with a smooth, hygrophanous cap 1.5–4 cm wide that fades from honey-brown to ochre as it dries. Gills are rusty brown and produce a rusty-brown spore print. Slender stem has a thin, often fragile ring and a fibrous, darker base.
- **Edibility:** **Deadly poisonous.** Contains amatoxins — the same lethal compounds found in the Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*). Causes delayed-onset liver and kidney failure.
- **Interesting fact:** It is dangerously easy to confuse with edible *Psilocybe* species and with the edible Honey Mushroom or Velvet Foot — there are documented fatalities from foragers misidentifying it as a "magic mushroom."

## Psilocybe cubensis

- **Scientific name:** *Psilocybe cubensis*
- **Habitat:** Pantropical and subtropical, native to the Americas and Southeast Asia; grows on the dung of large herbivores (especially cattle and water buffalo) and in well-fertilized pastures. Fruits in warm, humid conditions.
- **Appearance / ID:** Cap 1.5–8 cm, conic to convex becoming flat with a small umbo, golden-brown to pale tan, often with a paler margin. Gills are pale gray maturing to dark purple-black. Stem is whitish with a persistent membranous ring that catches purple-black spores. All parts bruise blue when handled, due to oxidation of psilocin.
- **Edibility:** **Psychoactive / hallucinogenic** — not eaten as food. Contains psilocybin and psilocin. Legal status varies widely (illegal in most countries; decriminalized or permitted in clinical contexts in some U.S. states and a few jurisdictions).
- **Interesting fact:** It is the most widely cultivated psilocybin mushroom in the world and is the species used in modern clinical trials investigating psilocybin therapy for treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, and end-of-life anxiety.

## White Truffle (Tuber magnatum)

- **Scientific name:** *Tuber magnatum* (the Italian or Alba white truffle)
- **Habitat:** A subterranean ascomycete native to a few specific regions of Italy (especially Piedmont/Alba, Tuscany, Umbria, Le Marche) and parts of Croatia, Slovenia, Serbia, and Hungary. Forms ectomycorrhizal associations with oak, hazel, poplar, and lime trees in calcareous soils.
- **Appearance / ID:** Irregular, lumpy underground fruiting body 3–12 cm across, with a smooth pale yellow-ochre to cream rind. Interior (gleba) is pale tan to pinkish-brown, marbled with thin white veins. Aroma is intense, complex, and garlicky-musky.
- **Edibility:** **Edible and the most prized of all truffles** — used raw, shaved over pasta, risotto, or eggs. Cooking destroys its volatile aromatic compounds.
- **Interesting fact:** It cannot be reliably cultivated — every white truffle on the market is wild-foraged, hunted with trained dogs (lagotti). A single large specimen sold for over US$300,000 at auction in 2007, making it gram-for-gram one of the most expensive foods on Earth.

## Golden Oyster (Pleurotus citrinopileatus)

- **Scientific name:** *Pleurotus citrinopileatus*
- **Habitat:** Native to eastern Russia, northern China, and Japan, growing on dead and dying hardwoods (especially elm). Now widely cultivated globally and increasingly recorded as naturalized/invasive in North American forests.
- **Appearance / ID:** Grows in striking bouquet-like clusters of 10–100 caps. Each cap is bright lemon-yellow, 2–6 cm wide, thin, and funnel-shaped at maturity. Gills are decurrent and white to cream, running down a short, eccentric stem. Spore print is pinkish-lilac.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** — pleasant nutty/cashew flavor when cooked thoroughly; can cause GI upset if undercooked. Texture is more delicate and crisp than the common gray oyster.
- **Interesting fact:** It is now considered an invasive species in parts of the U.S. Midwest and Northeast — escaped from cultivation kits, it is outcompeting native fungi on ash and elm logs and is one of the first widely cultivated mushrooms documented to "go wild."

## Ghost Fungus (Omphalotus nidiformis)

- **Scientific name:** *Omphalotus nidiformis*
- **Habitat:** Native to southern Australia and Tasmania (with related populations in India). Grows on living and dead trees, especially eucalyptus, in temperate forests; fruits in autumn and winter.
- **Appearance / ID:** Large, fan- or funnel-shaped overlapping caps up to 30 cm across, cream to grayish with brown, purple, or bluish tones, often with a darker center. Gills are decurrent, cream-white, and run down a short eccentric stem. Resembles an oyster mushroom in shape.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic.** Contains illudin S (the same toxin as the Jack-o'-Lantern, *O. olearius*). Causes severe vomiting and diarrhea but is not deadly.
- **Interesting fact:** It is bioluminescent — its gills emit a soft, eerie green glow in the dark via a luciferin-luciferase reaction. The glow is bright enough to read by in complete darkness and gave rise to Aboriginal Australian legends of spirit lights in the forest.

## Veiled Lady (Phallus indusiatus)

- **Scientific name:** *Phallus indusiatus* (also called the Bamboo Mushroom or Long-net Stinkhorn)
- **Habitat:** Tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, particularly common in southern Asia, Africa, the Americas, and Australia. Grows in rich humus, gardens, and bamboo groves, often after warm rains.
- **Appearance / ID:** Emerges from a white "egg," producing a tall white spongy stalk (10–25 cm) topped with a dark olive-brown, slimy, foul-smelling cap (the gleba). Its most distinctive feature is a delicate, lacy white "skirt" or indusium that hangs from beneath the cap nearly to the ground.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** when the gleba (slime) is washed off; the egg stage and dried stalk are used. Considered a delicacy in Chinese cuisine, prized for its crunchy texture.
- **Interesting fact:** The fetid smell attracts flies, which spread its spores — but in human food culture, the dried mushroom features in imperial Chinese banquets and was historically reserved for emperors. It can also grow at an astonishing rate, expanding several centimeters per hour.

## Devil's Urn (Urnula craterium)

- **Scientific name:** *Urnula craterium*
- **Habitat:** Eastern North America (and parts of Asia); grows on dead, fallen hardwood branches and buried wood, especially oak. Fruits in early spring (March–May), often before morels appear, signaling morel season.
- **Appearance / ID:** A deep, goblet- or urn-shaped ascomycete cup, 3–8 cm tall and 2–5 cm wide, with a tough, leathery wall. The interior is jet-black to dark brown and smooth, while the exterior is brownish-black and finely scurfy. Tapers to a short, often buried stem.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible** — not toxic in the dangerous sense, but tough, leathery, and considered without flavor; not used in cuisine.
- **Interesting fact:** When mature, the cups can audibly "puff" out clouds of spores when struck by raindrops or disturbed — a passive ballistic dispersal mechanism. It's also a useful phenological marker for foragers: where Devil's Urns appear, morels are likely soon to follow.

## False Morel (Gyromitra esculenta)

- **Scientific name:** *Gyromitra esculenta*
- **Habitat:** Northern Hemisphere temperate and boreal forests, especially under conifers (pine) on sandy soils, often near disturbed ground. Fruits in early spring, the same season as true morels.
- **Appearance / ID:** Cap is a reddish-brown, brain-like or saddle-shaped, irregularly lobed and convoluted mass — distinctly *not* honeycomb-pitted like a true morel. Stem is short, stout, whitish to pinkish, and chambered/stuffed (not cleanly hollow). Up to 10 cm tall and 15 cm wide.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic — potentially deadly.** Contains gyromitrin, which the body metabolizes into monomethylhydrazine (MMH) — a chemical also used in rocket fuel. Causes severe GI symptoms, hemolysis, neurological damage, and possible death.
- **Interesting fact:** Despite its toxicity, it is still eaten as a regional delicacy in Finland (sold under regulation as *korvasieni*) and parts of Eastern Europe after careful parboiling and venting — the toxin is volatile, but the fumes alone have poisoned cooks who lacked ventilation.

## Earthstar (Geastrum spp.)

- **Scientific name:** *Geastrum* spp. (genus comprising ~50 species, e.g. *G. triplex*, *G. saccatum*, *G. fornicatum*)
- **Habitat:** Worldwide distribution; grows on the ground in leaf litter, woodland duff, sandy soils, and humus-rich areas, often under hardwoods or conifers. Fruits late summer through autumn, with leathery remains persisting through winter.
- **Appearance / ID:** Begins as an onion-shaped ball, then the tough outer wall splits and peels back into 4–12 pointed, star-like rays, lifting and exposing a central spherical spore sac with an apical pore. Overall diameter 3–10 cm when expanded; colors range from pale tan to grayish-brown.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible** — not poisonous, but tough, fibrous, and woody; no culinary use.
- **Interesting fact:** Earthstars are a textbook example of biomechanical engineering in fungi: the rays are hygroscopic, opening when wet and closing when dry, while raindrops striking the spore sac act as bellows to puff spores through the apical pore — a phenomenon called "splash-cup" dispersal. *Geastrum fornicatum* even arches its rays to lift the spore sac off the ground for a better launch.
