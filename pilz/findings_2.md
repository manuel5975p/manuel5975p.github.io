# Findings — Batch 2 (Mushrooms 11–20)

## Texas Star (Chorioactis geaster)

- **Scientific name:** *Chorioactis geaster*
- **Habitat:** Extremely rare; known only from a few sites in central Texas (USA) and isolated regions of Japan (Kyushu and Shikoku, where it's called "kirinomitake"). Grows on dead/buried roots of cedar elm in Texas and dead oaks in Japan.
- **Appearance:** Begins as a dark brown, cigar-shaped, hairy capsule emerging from soil. At maturity it splits along longitudinal seams into 4–7 ray-like arms, exposing a salmon-pink to tan inner spore surface—resembling a star or exotic flower.
- **Edibility:** Inedible (not toxic, but tough; far too rare to harvest).
- **Interesting fact:** When the capsule splits, it produces an audible hiss as it forcibly discharges a visible cloud of spores—one of the few fungi known to make a sound during sporulation. Its disjunct Texas–Japan distribution is a long-standing biogeographical mystery.

## Death Cap (Amanita phalloides)

- **Scientific name:** *Amanita phalloides*
- **Habitat:** Native to Europe; invasively established across North America, Australia, and parts of South America. Forms ectomycorrhizal associations with hardwoods (especially oaks) and some conifers.
- **Appearance:** Smooth greenish-yellow to olive-bronze cap (5–15 cm), white gills, white stem with a skirt-like ring (annulus), and a prominent membranous sac (volva) at the base. Flesh white, with a faintly sweet/sickly odor when old.
- **Edibility:** Deadly poisonous. Responsible for roughly 90% of all fatal mushroom poisonings worldwide.
- **Interesting fact:** Contains amatoxins (chiefly α-amanitin), which inhibit RNA polymerase II and destroy liver and kidney cells. Symptoms are biphasic: severe GI distress 6–24 hours post-ingestion, a deceptive "false recovery," then catastrophic organ failure. Cooking, drying, and freezing do not destroy the toxin.

## Fairy Ring Champignon (Marasmius oreades)

- **Scientific name:** *Marasmius oreades*
- **Habitat:** Lawns, pastures, meadows, golf courses, and grassy roadsides throughout the temperate Northern Hemisphere. Forms expanding rings as the mycelium grows outward.
- **Appearance:** Small, tan to buff, bell-shaped to flat caps (2–5 cm), often with a central umbo. Widely spaced pale gills; tough, wiry, pliable stem that revives after drying. Sweet, almond-like odor.
- **Edibility:** Edible and choice, with a sweet flavor; popular in European cooking. CAUTION: easily confused with deadly *Clitocybe* species (e.g., *C. rivulosa*) sharing the same habitat.
- **Interesting fact:** The classic "fairy rings" of folklore—mushrooms in circles on lawns—are produced by this species' radially expanding mycelium. Some rings are estimated to be hundreds of years old and several meters across.

## Mycorrhizal Network ("Wood Wide Web")

- **Scientific name:** Not a single species; symbioses formed primarily by Glomeromycota (arbuscular) and Basidiomycota/Ascomycota (ectomycorrhizal) fungi with plant roots.
- **Habitat:** Virtually every terrestrial ecosystem—forests, grasslands, tundras, croplands. About 90% of vascular plant species form mycorrhizal partnerships.
- **Appearance:** Largely invisible underground hyphal networks. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath root tips with a fungal mantle (Hartig net visible microscopically); arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi penetrate root cells, forming tree-shaped arbuscules.
- **Edibility:** Not applicable as a unit; some fruiting bodies of network-forming species (chanterelles, truffles, porcini, matsutake) are highly prized edibles.
- **Interesting fact:** Suzanne Simard's research demonstrated that trees can transfer carbon, nitrogen, water, and even chemical defense signals between individuals via shared fungal hyphae—coining the "Wood Wide Web." Older "hub" or "mother" trees are thought to preferentially nurture nearby kin seedlings through these networks.

## Corn Smut / Huitlacoche (Ustilago maydis)

- **Scientific name:** *Ustilago maydis*
- **Habitat:** A biotrophic plant pathogen of maize and its wild relative teosinte. Found wherever corn is cultivated, worldwide.
- **Appearance:** Infects kernels, tassels, or stems and replaces them with swollen, silvery-gray to bluish-black galls that rupture to release dark olive-black powdery spores (teliospores). Galls range from golf-ball- to fist-sized.
- **Edibility:** Edible and considered a delicacy in Mexican cuisine—called *huitlacoche* or "Mexican truffle"—best harvested young while interior is still pale and moist. Used in quesadillas, soups, and tamales.
- **Interesting fact:** Despised as a destructive crop disease in the U.S. corn belt but deliberately cultivated and sold at premium prices in Mexico. Nutritionally superior to uninfected corn: higher in protein, lysine (a limiting amino acid in maize), and beta-glucans. Also a major model organism in molecular plant pathology.

## Matsutake (Tricholoma matsutake)

- **Scientific name:** *Tricholoma matsutake*
- **Habitat:** Ectomycorrhizal symbiont of pines—especially Japanese red pine (*Pinus densiflora*) in Japan; also Korea, China, and parts of northern Europe. Closely related species occur in North America (*T. magnivelare*).
- **Appearance:** Stout, firm mushroom with a brown, fibrillose-scaly cap (10–20 cm), white gills, and a thick white stem with a prominent membranous ring; below the ring the stem is sheathed with brown fibrils. Strong, distinctive spicy-sweet aroma.
- **Edibility:** Edible and highly prized; one of the most expensive mushrooms in the world.
- **Interesting fact:** Top-grade Japanese matsutake can fetch over USD 1,000/kg at peak season. Its aromatic compounds (notably 1-octen-3-ol and methyl cinnamate) are culturally iconic in Japan as a marker of autumn. Japanese harvests have collapsed (>95% decline since the 1940s) due to declining red pine forests, driving large imports from Korea, China, and the Pacific Northwest.

## Wrinkled Peach (Rhodotus palmatus)

- **Scientific name:** *Rhodotus palmatus*
- **Habitat:** Saprotroph on rotting hardwood logs—particularly elm and beech; widespread but uncommon to rare across temperate Europe, North America, North Africa, and parts of Asia.
- **Appearance:** Striking pinkish-orange to apricot cap (3–10 cm) with a deeply wrinkled, vein-like, gelatinous surface. Gills pale pink; stem off-center, often curved. Mature fruitbodies sometimes ooze blood-red droplets.
- **Edibility:** Generally considered inedible—bitter taste, rubbery texture, and too rare to harvest responsibly.
- **Interesting fact:** Listed on Red Lists of threatened fungi in several European countries because of its dependence on large-diameter dead elm wood, which has declined dramatically due to Dutch elm disease. Its reddish "tears" are produced through guttation and contain pigments unique among agarics.

## Birch Polypore (Fomitopsis betulina)

- **Scientific name:** *Fomitopsis betulina* (formerly *Piptoporus betulinus*)
- **Habitat:** Almost exclusively on dead or dying birch trees throughout the Northern Hemisphere. Causes brown rot.
- **Appearance:** Hoof- to kidney-shaped bracket fungus (5–25 cm wide), smooth pale brown to grayish upper surface with an inrolled rim; underside white with tiny pores. Flesh white, corky-tough.
- **Edibility:** Inedible due to bitter taste and tough texture, but non-toxic and traditionally used medicinally.
- **Interesting fact:** Ötzi the Iceman (the 5,300-year-old Copper Age mummy found in the Alps) carried pieces of birch polypore on a leather thong—likely used as an antiparasitic and antimicrobial. Contains piptamine, betulinic acid, and other compounds with documented antibacterial, antiviral, and anti-inflammatory activity. Also historically used as a strop for sharpening razors ("razor strop fungus") and as tinder.

## Honey Fungus (Armillaria mellea)

- **Scientific name:** *Armillaria mellea* (one of several similar Armillaria species)
- **Habitat:** Worldwide in temperate forests, parks, and gardens; both a saprotroph on dead wood and an aggressive parasite on living trees and shrubs. Spreads via black, shoelace-like rhizomorphs underground.
- **Appearance:** Honey-yellow to tawny-brown caps (3–15 cm), often with small dark scales near the center; whitish to cream gills running slightly down the stem; pale stem with a persistent cottony ring. Fruits in dense clumps at the bases of trees and stumps in autumn.
- **Edibility:** Edible only when thoroughly cooked; raw or undercooked specimens cause gastrointestinal upset, and some people are sensitive even to cooked specimens.
- **Interesting fact:** A single individual of *Armillaria ostoyae* (a close relative) in Oregon's Malheur National Forest covers about 9 km² (3.7 sq mi) and is estimated at 2,400+ years old—often cited as the largest and one of the oldest organisms on Earth. Honey fungus mycelium is also bioluminescent, producing the eerie green glow known as "foxfire."

## Stinkhorn (Phallus impudicus)

- **Scientific name:** *Phallus impudicus*
- **Habitat:** Common in woodlands, gardens, and mulched beds across Europe, North America, and elsewhere; saprotrophic on buried wood and rich humus.
- **Appearance:** Emerges from a white, gelatinous, egg-like structure ("witch's egg") in the soil. Mature fruitbody is a phallic, hollow white stalk (10–25 cm tall) topped with a thimble-like, pitted greenish-olive cap coated in dark, slimy, foul-smelling spore mass (gleba).
- **Edibility:** The immature "egg" stage is edible and considered a delicacy in parts of Germany and France ("Hexenei"); the mature, smelly fruitbody is inedible due to its revolting odor, though not toxic.
- **Interesting fact:** The carrion-like stench is an evolutionary adaptation to attract flies and other carrion-feeding insects, which then disperse spores externally on their bodies—an unusual departure from the wind-dispersal typical of most mushrooms. Charles Darwin's daughter Etty famously crusaded against them, collecting and burning specimens to protect Victorian morals.
