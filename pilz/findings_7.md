# Findings — Batch 7 (Mushrooms 61–70)

## Fly Agaric (Amanita muscaria)

- **Scientific name:** *Amanita muscaria*
- **Habitat:** Widespread across the Northern Hemisphere in temperate and boreal forests; forms ectomycorrhizal associations with birch, pine, spruce, and fir. Introduced to the Southern Hemisphere with conifer plantations.
- **Appearance:** Iconic bright red to orange-red cap (8–20 cm) covered with white to yellowish wart-like patches (remnants of the universal veil). White gills, white stem with a ring (annulus), and a bulbous base with concentric rings of volva remnants.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic / poisonous.** Contains the neurotoxins ibotenic acid and muscimol, which cause delirium, confusion, ataxia, nausea, and hallucinations. Rarely fatal in adults but dangerous, especially for children. It is sometimes parboiled and eaten in parts of Europe and Japan after careful detoxification.
- **Interesting fact:** The name "fly agaric" comes from the medieval practice of crushing caps in milk to attract and kill flies. It is also famously associated with Siberian shamanic rituals and pop culture — from fairy tales to Mario's Super Mushroom.

## Black Witch's Butter (Exidia glandulosa)

- **Scientific name:** *Exidia glandulosa*
- **Habitat:** Grows on dead and decaying hardwood branches and logs (especially oak) throughout temperate Europe and North America. Most visible after rain in autumn and winter.
- **Appearance:** A jelly fungus forming irregular, lobed, brain-like or button-shaped masses, typically 1–3 cm across but often coalescing into larger patches. Color is dark olive-black to pure black, with a glossy slimy surface when wet that shrivels to a thin black crust when dry.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible.** Not toxic, but rubbery, tasteless, and unpleasant in texture. No culinary value.
- **Interesting fact:** In folklore, witch's butter appearing on the gate or door of one's home was considered evidence that a witch had cursed the household; the only remedy was to pierce the fungus with pins or burn it to harm the witch.

## Rusty Porecrust (Phellinus ferruginosus)

- **Scientific name:** *Phellinus ferruginosus* (also seen as *Fuscoporia ferruginosa*)
- **Habitat:** A resupinate (crust-like) wood-decay fungus growing flat on the underside of dead hardwood branches and logs, particularly hazel, oak, and other broadleaves. Common across Europe and parts of North America.
- **Appearance:** Forms a thin, tough, rust-brown to cinnamon-brown crust tightly attached to wood. Pore surface is finely porous (4–6 pores per mm) with a velvety dark brown margin. No stem or distinct cap — just a sprawling patch that can extend tens of centimeters.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible.** Woody and tough; not considered for consumption. Not known to be toxic but indigestible.
- **Interesting fact:** *Phellinus* species are white-rot fungi that break down lignin in wood, and several relatives are studied for their medicinal compounds and as bioindicators of old-growth forest health.

## Bioluminescent Panellus (Panellus stipticus)

- **Scientific name:** *Panellus stipticus*
- **Habitat:** Grows in overlapping clusters on dead and decaying hardwood logs and stumps (especially oak, beech, and birch) in temperate forests across North America, Europe, Asia, and Australasia.
- **Appearance:** Small kidney- or fan-shaped caps, 1–3 cm across, tan to ochre-brown with a finely scurfy surface. Gills are pale orange-buff, closely spaced, and run down to a short, off-center lateral stem. Tough, leathery flesh.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible / mildly toxic.** Acrid and astringent (the species name *stipticus* refers to its mouth-puckering taste). Reported to cause gastrointestinal upset.
- **Interesting fact:** North American populations are strongly bioluminescent — gills glow green in the dark via a luciferin–luciferase reaction (a phenomenon called "foxfire"). Curiously, European populations of the same species do not glow, making it a classic example of regional biochemical variation.

## White Button Mushroom (Agaricus bisporus)

- **Scientific name:** *Agaricus bisporus*
- **Habitat:** In the wild, found in grasslands, meadows, and compost-rich soils across Europe and North America. Today, primarily cultivated commercially worldwide on pasteurized straw-and-manure compost.
- **Appearance:** Smooth white to pale brown cap (5–10 cm), hemispherical when young and flattening with age. Gills start pink and turn dark chocolate-brown as spores mature. Short, thick white stem with a thin ring. Cremini and Portobello are the same species at later maturity stages.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** — one of the most consumed mushrooms in the world. Best cooked, since raw consumption is discouraged due to small amounts of agaritine (a hydrazine compound).
- **Interesting fact:** *A. bisporus* accounts for roughly 90% of mushrooms produced and consumed in the United States, and the common white, brown (cremini), and Portobello mushrooms in supermarkets are simply different ages and strains of the very same species.

## Cauliflower Mushroom (Sparassis crispa)

- **Scientific name:** *Sparassis crispa*
- **Habitat:** Parasitic and saprotrophic on the roots and base of conifers, particularly pines, spruces, and Douglas-fir. Found in temperate forests across Europe, North America, and East Asia, fruiting in late summer and autumn.
- **Appearance:** A large, distinctive, cream to pale-yellow fruiting body resembling a head of cauliflower or frilly sea sponge — composed of countless wavy, ribbon-like, flattened lobes arising from a single central base. Often 20–40 cm wide and weighing several kilograms.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** and prized as a choice gourmet mushroom. Pleasant nutty flavor with a crunchy texture; requires careful cleaning because debris collects between the folds.
- **Interesting fact:** *Sparassis crispa* causes a brown-rot heart disease in living conifer trees, and a single individual can fruit from the same tree's root system year after year. It is also being researched for beta-glucan content with potential immune-modulating properties.

## Snow Fungus (Tremella fuciformis)

- **Scientific name:** *Tremella fuciformis*
- **Habitat:** A parasitic jelly fungus that grows on the mycelium of *Annulohypoxylon* (and related ascomycete) crust fungi colonizing dead hardwood. Native to tropical and subtropical regions worldwide; widely cultivated in China.
- **Appearance:** Translucent to pure-white, gelatinous fruiting body composed of thin, frilly, leaf-like lobes radiating outward, resembling a sea anemone or a flower-shaped puff of icing, typically 5–15 cm across. Becomes brittle when dry and rehydrates fully when soaked.
- **Edibility:** **Edible.** Used extensively in Chinese cuisine, especially in sweet soups (*tang*), desserts, and tonics. Mild flavor with a notably crunchy-gelatinous texture.
- **Interesting fact:** Known as "silver ear" (银耳, *yín ěr*) in Chinese, it has been prized for over 2,000 years in traditional Chinese medicine and beauty regimens. Imperial concubine Yang Guifei reportedly used it for skin care, and it is still marketed today as a "natural hyaluronic acid" because it can hold up to 500 times its weight in water.

## Slippery Jack (Suillus luteus)

- **Scientific name:** *Suillus luteus*
- **Habitat:** Forms ectomycorrhizal associations almost exclusively with pines (especially Scots pine). Native to Eurasia but widely introduced wherever pines are planted (North America, South America, southern Africa, Australia, New Zealand).
- **Appearance:** A bolete with a chocolate-brown to chestnut, distinctly slimy/slippery cap (5–12 cm) — diagnostic feature. Underside has small yellow pores rather than gills. Stem is whitish-yellow with dark glandular dots above and a conspicuous purplish-brown ring (annulus), rare among boletes.
- **Edibility:** **Edible.** Popular in Eastern Europe and Italy, but the slimy cap cuticle should be peeled before cooking, as it can cause gastrointestinal discomfort and is unpleasant in texture. Drying or pickling is common.
- **Interesting fact:** *Suillus luteus* has hitchhiked around the world with pine plantations and is now considered an invasive symbiont in some regions. Its tolerance of heavy-metal-polluted soils has even been studied for use in mycoremediation of contaminated mining sites.

## King Oyster (Pleurotus eryngii)

- **Scientific name:** *Pleurotus eryngii*
- **Habitat:** Native to Mediterranean Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, and Central Asia, where it is weakly parasitic/saprotrophic on the roots of umbellifer plants — most famously *Eryngium campestre* (field eryngo), giving it its species name. Now cultivated worldwide.
- **Appearance:** Unlike other oyster mushrooms, it has a thick, meaty, cylindrical white stem (its main edible part) topped by a relatively small tan to brown convex cap. Decurrent cream-white gills run down the stem. Whole specimen 8–20 cm tall, firm and dense.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** and considered choice. Mild umami, savory flavor with a firm, scallop-like texture when cooked; one of the few oyster mushrooms whose stem is the prized portion.
- **Interesting fact:** Because of its dense, fibrous stem texture, the King Oyster is widely used in vegan cuisine as a substitute for scallops, calamari, or pulled pork — sliced rounds seared in butter are remarkably similar to seafood, and shredded stems mimic meat fibers convincingly.

## Poison Pie (Hebeloma crustuliniforme)

- **Scientific name:** *Hebeloma crustuliniforme*
- **Habitat:** Ectomycorrhizal with a wide variety of trees, especially birch, oak, beech, and spruce. Common in mixed and deciduous woodlands across Europe and North America, fruiting in late summer through autumn.
- **Appearance:** Pale tan to pinkish-buff, smooth and slightly viscid cap (4–9 cm), convex then flattening, often with a paler margin. Pale clay-brown gills that "weep" tiny droplets when young (drying into dark spots). Stem is whitish, finely scurfy at the apex, without a distinct ring. Strong radish-like odor and bitter taste.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic.** Contains gastrointestinal irritants causing vomiting, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea within hours of ingestion. Not life-threatening in healthy adults, but seriously unpleasant.
- **Interesting fact:** The "weeping" gills (a feature called *guttation*) are diagnostic for many *Hebeloma* species, and the bitter, raw-radish smell is so reliable that experienced foragers can identify the genus from a sniff before even looking at the gills — a useful warning given how unremarkable the cap looks compared to common edible species.
