# Findings — Batch 5 (Mushrooms 41–50)

## Golden Coral Fungus

- **Scientific name:** *Ramaria aurea*
- **Habitat:** Found in deciduous and coniferous forests across Europe and North America, growing on the ground in leaf litter, often in summer and autumn. Forms mycorrhizal associations with trees, particularly beech and oak.
- **Appearance:** Strikingly resembles a piece of marine coral, with a densely branched, upright fruiting body reaching 5–15 cm tall. Color is a vibrant golden-yellow to ochre-yellow, sometimes with paler tips. Branches are cylindrical, repeatedly forked, arising from a thick, whitish stem base. Flesh is firm and brittle.
- **Edibility:** Generally considered **inedible** or of poor quality; can cause gastrointestinal upset (laxative effect) in some individuals. Easily confused with toxic *Ramaria* species.
- **Interesting fact:** *Ramaria* species are notoriously difficult to identify even for experts—microscopic spore examination is often required, and the genus contains both edible and mildly toxic species that look nearly identical in the field.

## Copper Spike

- **Scientific name:** *Chroogomphus rutilus*
- **Habitat:** Grows under pine trees in coniferous and mixed forests across Europe, Asia, and North America. Appears from late summer through autumn, often in sandy soils.
- **Appearance:** Cap is conical to convex, 3–12 cm wide, with a distinctive coppery-brown, reddish-brown, or wine-red color and a slightly slimy or sticky surface when wet. Gills are widely spaced, decurrent, and turn dark olive-black with maturity. Stem is tapered, often pointed at the base, matching the cap color.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** but considered mediocre in quality; flesh becomes purplish when cooked. Some sources warn of heavy metal accumulation.
- **Interesting fact:** Recent research has revealed that *Chroogomphus* species are actually **mycoparasites**—they hijack the mycelium of *Suillus* mushrooms to obtain nutrients, making their relationship with pines indirect rather than truly mycorrhizal.

## Beefsteak Fungus

- **Scientific name:** *Fistulina hepatica*
- **Habitat:** A parasitic and saprobic bracket fungus growing on living and dead hardwood trees, especially oak and sweet chestnut. Widespread in Europe, North America, Australia, and parts of Africa, fruiting from late summer to autumn.
- **Appearance:** Truly resembles a slab of raw meat or liver—tongue-shaped, semicircular bracket 10–25 cm across, blood-red to liver-brown, with a moist, gelatinous upper surface. When cut, the flesh is marbled with reddish veins and exudes a blood-like reddish juice. The underside has tiny, separate, tube-like pores (unique among bracket fungi).
- **Edibility:** **Edible** when young, with a slightly sour, citrus-like taste; often eaten raw in thin slices like carpaccio or cooked.
- **Interesting fact:** Beefsteak fungus causes a desirable form of "brown oak" staining in oak timber, turning the wood a rich reddish-brown that is highly prized by furniture makers and woodworkers.

## Radiotrophic Fungi

- **Scientific name:** *Cladosporium sphaerospermum* (also *Cryptococcus neoformans*, *Wangiella dermatitidis*)
- **Habitat:** Famously discovered thriving inside the highly radioactive sarcophagus of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant Reactor No. 4. Also found on the International Space Station, in Antarctic rocks, and in other extreme environments globally.
- **Appearance:** Microscopic black molds, not forming visible mushrooms. Colonies appear as dark, sooty, or olive-black patches due to high concentrations of melanin, which gives them their characteristic coloring.
- **Edibility:** **Not applicable / inedible**—these are microscopic molds and some species can be opportunistic pathogens in immunocompromised individuals.
- **Interesting fact:** These fungi appear to perform "radiosynthesis"—using melanin to convert ionizing radiation (gamma rays) into chemical energy, similar to how plants use chlorophyll for photosynthesis. They actually grow **toward** radiation sources and are being studied as potential biological radiation shields for astronauts on Mars missions.

## Sulfur Tuft

- **Scientific name:** *Hypholoma fasciculare*
- **Habitat:** One of the most common wood-decay fungi worldwide, growing in dense, clustered tufts on dead and decaying hardwood and coniferous stumps, logs, and buried roots. Fruits from spring through late autumn across the Northern Hemisphere.
- **Appearance:** Small to medium caps (2–7 cm), bright sulfur-yellow to greenish-yellow, often with an orange-brown center. Gills are crowded, starting sulfur-yellow then turning greenish, and finally purplish-brown as spores mature. Stems are slender, yellow, often curved, and grow in dense bundles.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic**—contains fasciculol toxins that cause severe gastrointestinal distress, vomiting, diarrhea, and in rare cases more serious symptoms including paralysis. Has caused fatalities, particularly in Japan.
- **Interesting fact:** Sulfur Tuft has an intensely bitter taste that usually deters consumption, but it is dangerously similar in appearance to the edible Sheathed Woodtuft (*Kuehneromyces mutabilis*) and Honey Fungus, leading to occasional misidentification poisonings.

## Silver Leaf Fungus

- **Scientific name:** *Chondrostereum purpureum*
- **Habitat:** A widespread plant pathogen growing on dead and living hardwood trees, particularly fruit trees of the *Prunus* genus (cherry, plum, peach). Found throughout temperate regions worldwide; fruits year-round but most visible in autumn and winter.
- **Appearance:** Forms thin, wavy, overlapping bracket-like fruiting bodies or resupinate crusts on bark. Upper surface is hairy and grayish-white to brownish; the underside (fertile surface) is smooth and a striking purple to lilac-violet color when fresh, fading to brown when dry.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible**—too thin and tough to be of culinary interest, and it is a serious plant pathogen.
- **Interesting fact:** This fungus causes "silver leaf disease" in fruit trees—it doesn't actually invade the leaves, but its toxins cause the leaves to develop a silvery sheen due to air pockets forming under the leaf cuticle. It is also being researched as a **biological herbicide** (mycoherbicide) for controlling unwanted hardwood regrowth.

## King Coal Fungus / Coal Fungus

- **Scientific name:** *Daldinia concentrica* (also called King Alfred's Cakes or Cramp Balls)
- **Habitat:** Grows on dead hardwood, particularly ash and beech, throughout Europe, North America, and other temperate regions. Fruits year-round but is most easily spotted in winter when leaves have fallen.
- **Appearance:** Hard, hemispherical to globular black balls, 2–7 cm in diameter, attached to wood. The surface is shiny black when mature (purplish-brown when young). When cut open, the interior reveals distinctive concentric silvery-grey and black rings, like tree growth rings.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible**—the flesh is hard, woody, and carbonaceous, with no culinary value.
- **Interesting fact:** Despite being inedible, *Daldinia concentrica* is highly prized by bushcrafters and survivalists as **natural tinder**—when dried, it catches a spark from flint and steel exceptionally well and smolders for a long time, allowing fire to be transported. This use dates back to prehistoric times.

## Leaden Entoloma

- **Scientific name:** *Entoloma sinuatum* (syn. *Entoloma lividum*)
- **Habitat:** Grows on the ground in deciduous forests, particularly under oak, beech, and lime trees, often on calcareous (limestone-rich) soils. Found across Europe and eastern North America, fruiting in summer and autumn.
- **Appearance:** Large, robust mushroom with a cap 6–20 cm wide, ivory-white to pale grey or lead-grey, smooth and silky, often becoming irregular and wavy with age. Gills are notably yellowish at first, turning salmon-pink as the angular spores mature. Stem is stout, white, and fibrous. Has a distinctive smell of cucumber or rancid flour.
- **Edibility:** **Toxic**—causes one of the most common mushroom poisonings in Europe, with severe gastrointestinal symptoms (violent vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration) lasting 2–3 days. Rarely fatal but extremely unpleasant.
- **Interesting fact:** Often called "The Livid Entoloma" or "The Great Pretender" because it is frequently mistaken for several edible species, including the Miller (*Clitopilus prunulus*), St. George's Mushroom (*Calocybe gambosa*), and even young porcini—making it one of the most dangerous mimics in European forests.

## Mycena chlorophos

- **Scientific name:** *Mycena chlorophos*
- **Habitat:** A tropical and subtropical species growing on decaying wood, fallen branches, and leaf litter in humid forests of Japan, Taiwan, Southeast Asia, Australia, Brazil, and some Pacific islands. Fruits during the rainy season.
- **Appearance:** Small, delicate mushroom with a translucent, pale grey-brown to pale green hemispherical cap (1–3 cm wide) on a thin, fragile stem (up to 6 cm). The cap is sticky when wet and somewhat striated. By day, it looks unremarkable; at night, the gills, cap, and stem glow with a soft, eerie green-blue light.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible**—too small, fragile, and considered of unknown edibility; not consumed.
- **Interesting fact:** *Mycena chlorophos* is one of the brightest **bioluminescent mushrooms** known. Its glow is produced by the enzyme luciferase reacting with luciferin and oxygen, peaking in intensity for only a few hours after fruiting. The phenomenon, called "foxfire" or *kitsunebi* in Japan, may attract insects that help disperse spores.

## Dead Man's Fingers

- **Scientific name:** *Xylaria polymorpha*
- **Habitat:** A saprobic fungus growing on rotting hardwood stumps and buried roots, especially beech, in deciduous forests across Europe, North America, and Asia. Fruits year-round but is most conspicuous in late summer through autumn.
- **Appearance:** Truly macabre—forms upright, finger-like or club-shaped projections (3–10 cm tall, 1–3 cm thick) emerging from the ground or wood, often in clusters that resemble a corpse's hand reaching from the soil. Young specimens are pale grey-blue with whitish tips; mature ones turn black, lumpy, and crusty. Interior flesh is white and tough.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible**—the flesh is extremely tough, woody, and devoid of culinary value.
- **Interesting fact:** *Xylaria polymorpha* is unusual among fungi in that it produces two types of spores at different life stages: asexual conidia in spring (giving young fingers their dusty, pale appearance) and sexual ascospores released slowly over many months from the mature black bodies, an exceptionally long fruiting cycle for any fungus.
