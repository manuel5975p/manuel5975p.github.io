# Findings — Batch 4 (Mushrooms 31–40)

## Lobster Mushroom (Hypomyces lactifluorum on host)

- **Scientific name:** *Hypomyces lactifluorum* (parasitic ascomycete) growing on host mushrooms, typically *Russula brevipes* or *Lactifluus piperatus*.
- **Habitat:** Coniferous and mixed forests across North America, late summer through autumn, often partially buried in duff where its host species grow.
- **Appearance:** A bright orange-to-red, pimply crust completely envelops and deforms the host mushroom, making it resemble cooked lobster shell. The flesh inside is firm, white, and dense, with a hollowed or twisted shape because the parasite halts the host's normal development.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** and highly prized; firm texture and seafood-like aroma. Caution: only eat well-identified specimens, since the host could theoretically be a toxic *Russula* — verified poisoning cases are extremely rare.
- **Interesting fact:** It's technically two organisms in one — you eat the parasite *and* its host, fused into a single edible body.

## Rose Comb (Rose-comb mushroom — mutated/abnormal Agaricus)

- **Scientific name:** *Agaricus bisporus* (and related *Agaricus* species) exhibiting "rose-comb" mutation; not a separate species.
- **Habitat:** Commercial mushroom farms, plus lawns, gardens, and compost-rich soils where button or portobello mushrooms are cultivated.
- **Appearance:** A developmental abnormality where gill tissue erupts onto the top of the cap, forming pinkish-brown, crested, brain- or coral-like folds resembling a rooster's comb. Normal cap structure is partly or completely replaced by exposed hymenium.
- **Edibility:** **Edible** — same as a normal Agaricus; the mutation is purely cosmetic. Commercial growers usually discard rose-combs because they look unappealing and are hard to clean.
- **Interesting fact:** The deformity is triggered by environmental stress — exposure to volatile hydrocarbons (diesel fumes, oil leaks, certain solvents) during pin formation can induce rose-comb in entire crops, making it a useful bioindicator of contamination.

## Humongous Fungus (Armillaria ostoyae — Oregon's giant)

- **Scientific name:** *Armillaria ostoyae* (also called *Armillaria solidipes*).
- **Habitat:** Coniferous forests across the Northern Hemisphere; the famous "Humongous Fungus" lives in the Malheur National Forest, Oregon, infecting Douglas-fir and other conifers.
- **Appearance:** Honey-colored to brown fruiting bodies in clusters at tree bases in autumn — caps with darker scales, white spore print, persistent ring on the stipe, fibrous flesh. Most of the organism is an underground network of black rhizomorphs ("shoestrings") and white mycelial fans under bark.
- **Edibility:** **Edible when thoroughly cooked** but causes gastrointestinal upset in some people; never eat raw or undercooked. It's a serious tree pathogen (cause of armillaria root rot).
- **Interesting fact:** The Oregon specimen covers ~2,385 acres (~9.6 km²), weighs an estimated 7,500–35,000 tons, and is roughly 2,400–8,650 years old — arguably the largest and one of the oldest living organisms on Earth.

## Ringless Honey Mushroom (Desarmillaria caespitosa / tabescens)

- **Scientific name:** *Desarmillaria caespitosa* (formerly *Armillaria tabescens*).
- **Habitat:** Dense clusters at the base of hardwoods (especially oak) and from buried roots across the eastern and southern United States, Europe, and East Asia. Common in late summer and fall.
- **Appearance:** Honey-yellow to tawny-brown caps 3–10 cm wide with fine erect scales near the center, decurrent whitish gills, fibrous tapering stems fused at the base, and — diagnostically — **no ring (annulus)** on the stem. Spore print is white.
- **Edibility:** **Edible when thoroughly cooked**, but causes GI distress in a noticeable minority of people; some sources list it as inedible. Always cook well and try a small portion first.
- **Interesting fact:** Like its ringed cousin *Armillaria*, it's a destructive root-rot pathogen and is bioluminescent — its rhizomorphs and sometimes its gills produce a faint green glow known as "foxfire."

## Giant Puffball (Calvatia gigantea)

- **Scientific name:** *Calvatia gigantea*.
- **Habitat:** Meadows, pastures, deciduous woods, and roadside edges in temperate North America and Europe; appears summer to autumn, often in fairy rings.
- **Appearance:** A massive white, smooth, leathery ball, typically 20–80 cm across (occasionally over 1 m), looking like a soccer ball or even a sheep at a distance. Lacks a distinct stem; attached to the ground by a small cord. Interior is solid, pure white, marshmallow-textured when young; turns yellow then olive-brown and powdery (spores) with age.
- **Edibility:** **Edible and choice when young and pure white inside.** Once the flesh shows any yellow or green tint it's inedible (and may upset the stomach). Always slice through to confirm uniform white interior and rule out the deadly *Amanita* "egg" stage.
- **Interesting fact:** A mature giant puffball can release an estimated 7 trillion spores; if every spore germinated and produced another, the resulting mass would exceed the volume of the Earth in just two generations.

## Horn of Plenty (Craterellus cornucopioides)

- **Scientific name:** *Craterellus cornucopioides*.
- **Habitat:** Mossy beds and leaf litter under hardwoods (especially beech and oak) in temperate forests of Europe, North America, and Asia. Late summer to autumn; often hidden, growing in dense troops.
- **Appearance:** A thin, deeply funnel- to trumpet-shaped fruiting body 3–10 cm tall, hollow all the way to the base. Outer (fertile) surface smooth to slightly wrinkled and ash-gray; inner upper surface dark gray-brown to nearly black, often scaly. No true gills.
- **Edibility:** **Edible and excellent**, prized in French cuisine as *trompette de la mort* despite the morbid name. Dries beautifully and intensifies in flavor — rich, smoky, almost truffle-like.
- **Interesting fact:** Despite the ominous nickname "trumpet of death," the name refers to its color and its tendency to appear around All Saints' Day, not to any toxicity — there are no known dangerous lookalikes.

## Turkey Tail (Trametes versicolor)

- **Scientific name:** *Trametes versicolor* (formerly *Coriolus versicolor*).
- **Habitat:** One of the most common bracket fungi worldwide; overlapping shelves on dead hardwood logs, stumps, and branches, year-round in temperate forests.
- **Appearance:** Thin, leathery, semicircular to fan-shaped brackets 2–10 cm across, with concentric zones of contrasting colors — brown, tan, gray, white, blue, even greenish — alternating with a velvety to silky texture. Underside is a white-to-cream **pore** surface (not gills) with very tiny, regular pores (3–8 per mm).
- **Edibility:** **Inedible** as food (too tough/leathery), but **medicinal**: source of the polysaccharide-K (PSK / Krestin) and PSP compounds used as adjunct cancer therapies in Japan and China and studied for immune-modulating effects.
- **Interesting fact:** Look-alikes such as the "false turkey tail" (*Stereum ostrea*) lack pores on the underside — a quick flip is the reliable ID test.

## Black Trumpet (Craterellus cornucopioides — same/related)

- **Scientific name:** *Craterellus cornucopioides* (also *C. fallax* in eastern North America, considered by many to be the same taxon).
- **Habitat:** Damp, mossy hardwood forests, especially under oak, beech, and hemlock; from soil among leaf litter, often near streams. Summer through autumn in temperate regions globally.
- **Appearance:** Identical in form to the European Horn of Plenty: jet-black to charcoal-gray hollow funnels 3–12 cm tall, thin-fleshed, wavy-edged, with a smooth-to-wrinkled outer fertile surface. Dark color makes them notoriously hard to spot against forest duff.
- **Edibility:** **Edible and gourmet** — one of the most flavorful wild mushrooms; deeply aromatic, almost smoky, and excellent dried.
- **Interesting fact:** Black trumpets often fruit in the same patches year after year and can be encouraged by spreading trimmings — but their best secret is camouflage: once you spot one, your eyes adjust and dozens suddenly appear at your feet.

## Collared Earthstar (Geastrum triplex)

- **Scientific name:** *Geastrum triplex*.
- **Habitat:** Rich humus and leaf litter in deciduous forests (often beech) across temperate regions worldwide; fruits in late summer and autumn.
- **Appearance:** Starts as an onion-shaped underground "bulb" 3–5 cm wide. At maturity the thick outer wall splits into 4–8 fleshy, recurving rays that often crack to leave a distinctive saucer- or collar-like cup beneath the central spore sac. The inner sac is gray-brown, papery, with a single fringed pore (peristome) at the top through which spores puff out.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible** — not toxic, but tough, leathery, and flavorless.
- **Interesting fact:** The largest *Geastrum* species, it uses raindrops as a mechanical bellows — each drop hitting the spore sac compresses it and ejects a visible puff of spores through the apical pore, a strategy called "ballistospory by raindrop."

## Chaga (Inonotus obliquus)

- **Scientific name:** *Inonotus obliquus*.
- **Habitat:** Parasite of living birch trees (occasionally alder, beech, hornbeam) in cold-temperate and boreal forests of Russia, Northern Europe, Korea, Canada, and the northern United States.
- **Appearance:** A hard, irregular, charcoal-black, deeply cracked sterile mass ("conk") protruding from the trunk, 10–40+ cm across, looking like burnt wood or a chunk of coal. Inside, the flesh is rusty orange-brown and corky. The actual fruiting body is rarely seen — it forms under the bark after the host tree dies.
- **Edibility:** **Inedible** as a culinary mushroom (woody and bitter), but **medicinal**: traditionally simmered into a tea in Siberia and Northern Europe; rich in melanin, betulinic acid, and antioxidants, and studied for immunomodulatory and anti-tumor activity.
- **Interesting fact:** Chaga concentrates betulin and betulinic acid from its birch host — compounds the tree itself produces as a defense — making chaga essentially a fungal extractor and reservoir of birch's own bioactive chemistry.
