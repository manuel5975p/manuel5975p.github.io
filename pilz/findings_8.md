# Findings — Batch 8 (Mushrooms 71–80)

## Split Gill (Schizophyllum commune)

- **Scientific name:** *Schizophyllum commune*
- **Habitat:** One of the most widely distributed fungi on Earth, found on every continent except Antarctica. Grows on decaying hardwood logs, branches, and stumps in temperate, tropical, and subtropical forests.
- **Appearance:** Small (1–4 cm), fan- or shell-shaped fruiting body, white to grayish, densely hairy or fuzzy on the upper surface. The underside has distinctive "split" gills that appear longitudinally divided — each gill is actually two folds that separate when dry and re-fuse when moist.
- **Edibility:** Technically edible but extremely tough and leathery; considered inedible in Western cuisine. It is, however, eaten in parts of Mexico, India, and Southeast Asia.
- **Interesting fact:** *Schizophyllum commune* has more than 23,000 distinct mating types (sexes), the highest known number of biological sexes of any organism.

## Puffball (Lycoperdon perlatum)

- **Scientific name:** *Lycoperdon perlatum* (Common Puffball / Gem-studded Puffball)
- **Habitat:** Widespread in woodlands, meadows, pastures, and along trails throughout the Northern Hemisphere; fruits in summer and autumn, often in clusters on the ground or rotting wood.
- **Appearance:** Pear- or club-shaped, 2.5–6 cm tall, white to grayish-brown, covered in small pyramidal warts/spines that rub off to leave a mesh-like pattern. Mature specimens develop a pore at the top through which spores are released. Interior is firm and pure white when young, turning yellow then olive-brown and powdery.
- **Edibility:** Edible when young and the interior is uniformly pure white. Once the flesh yellows or browns, it becomes inedible. Caution: must not be confused with young *Amanita* "eggs," which can be deadly.
- **Interesting fact:** A single mature puffball can release up to 7 trillion spores, dispersed in puffs when raindrops strike the apical pore.

## Scarlet Caterpillarclub (Cordyceps militaris)

- **Scientific name:** *Cordyceps militaris*
- **Habitat:** Widespread in temperate regions of Europe, Asia, and North America; found in grasslands, mossy areas, and woodland leaf litter where it parasitizes buried lepidopteran (moth and butterfly) pupae and larvae.
- **Appearance:** Bright orange to scarlet club-shaped fruiting bodies, 2–8 cm tall, emerging singly from the soil above an infected, mummified insect host. The upper fertile portion is finely roughened (covered in perithecia) while the stipe is paler.
- **Edibility:** Edible and widely cultivated commercially as a medicinal mushroom. Used extensively in traditional Chinese medicine and as a nutritional supplement.
- **Interesting fact:** Considered a more sustainable substitute for the rare *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* of the Tibetan plateau. It produces cordycepin, a compound under active study for antiviral and anticancer properties.

## Hexagonal-pored Polypore (Neofavolus alveolaris)

- **Scientific name:** *Neofavolus alveolaris* (formerly *Polyporus alveolaris* / *Favolus alveolaris*)
- **Habitat:** Found on dead hardwood branches and twigs (especially oak, hickory, and elm) in deciduous forests across North America, Europe, and Asia; fruits spring through early summer.
- **Appearance:** Kidney- or fan-shaped cap, 2–10 cm wide, yellow-orange to reddish-brown with fine appressed scales, fading with age. The most distinctive feature is the underside: large, conspicuously hexagonal/diamond-shaped pores arranged in a honeycomb pattern, radially elongated. Stipe is short, lateral, or absent.
- **Edibility:** Considered inedible due to its tough, leathery flesh, though not toxic.
- **Interesting fact:** Its strikingly geometric pore pattern makes it one of the most easily identified polypores in the field — the hexagonal pores resemble a tiny natural honeycomb and are unique among common North American polypores.

## Conical Waxcap (Hygrocybe conica)

- **Scientific name:** *Hygrocybe conica* (Witch's Hat)
- **Habitat:** Grows in unimproved grasslands, mossy lawns, dunes, heathlands, and open woodlands throughout temperate regions worldwide; appears summer to late autumn.
- **Appearance:** Sharply conical cap, 2–5 cm wide, brilliantly colored in shades of scarlet, orange, or yellow when young. Surface is slightly greasy. The defining feature is that all parts (cap, gills, stem) bruise and age jet black, giving older specimens a charred appearance. Gills are pale yellow and free; stem is fibrous and often twisted.
- **Edibility:** Generally regarded as inedible or mildly toxic; some sources report gastrointestinal upset. Best avoided.
- **Interesting fact:** Waxcaps are indicator species of ancient, unfertilized grasslands ("waxcap grasslands"), increasingly rare conservation priorities in Europe. The dramatic blackening reaction is unusual among gilled mushrooms.

## Turkey Tail (Trametes versicolor)

- **Scientific name:** *Trametes versicolor*
- **Habitat:** One of the most common shelf fungi worldwide; grows in overlapping tiers on dead hardwood logs, stumps, and fallen branches in temperate forests across all continents (except Antarctica).
- **Appearance:** Thin, leathery, semicircular or fan-shaped brackets, 3–10 cm wide, displaying striking concentric zones of varying colors — browns, blues, grays, oranges, blacks, and whites. The arc patterns often follow logarithmic / Fibonacci-like spacing, producing a turkey-tail appearance. Underside has tiny white pores (3–8 per mm) — distinguishing it from look-alikes like the false turkey tail (*Stereum*), which has a smooth underside.
- **Edibility:** Too tough to eat, but widely consumed as a medicinal tea or extract.
- **Interesting fact:** Contains polysaccharide-K (PSK) and PSP, compounds approved in Japan as adjunct cancer therapy. It is among the most-studied medicinal mushrooms globally.

## Mycelial Mat (general fungal mycelium)

- **Scientific name:** N/A — refers collectively to the vegetative network of any fungus (e.g., *Armillaria*, *Pleurotus*, *Mycena* spp.)
- **Habitat:** Soils, leaf litter, decaying wood, and root systems virtually everywhere on Earth — in forests, grasslands, deserts, and even Antarctic dry valleys. Mycelium is the dominant living biomass in many forest soils.
- **Appearance:** A diffuse white, cream, or pale yellow web of fine thread-like hyphae forming sheets, films, or rope-like rhizomorphs that ramify through substrate. Visible when leaf litter or bark is lifted. Some species produce visible bioluminescence.
- **Edibility:** Not consumed directly; nutritional/medicinal mycelium of certain cultivated species (e.g., *Hericium*, *Cordyceps*) is sold as supplements.
- **Interesting fact:** A single *Armillaria ostoyae* mycelium in Oregon's Malheur National Forest covers ~9.6 km² and is estimated at 2,400+ years old — making it the largest and one of the oldest living organisms on Earth. Mycelial networks also form symbiotic "wood wide web" connections between trees.

## Roundhead Mushroom (Stropharia coronilla)

- **Scientific name:** *Stropharia coronilla* (Garland Roundhead)
- **Habitat:** Grows in lawns, pastures, parks, dunes, and grassy roadsides across Europe, North America, and parts of Asia and Australia; fruits late summer to autumn, often after rain.
- **Appearance:** Small to medium hemispheric-to-convex cap, 2–6 cm wide, smooth and pale yellow to ochre, slightly viscid when wet. Gills are crowded, starting pale gray-violet and maturing to dark purple-brown. The stem bears a distinct membranous ring (annulus) with radial striations on its upper surface — a key feature of *Stropharia*.
- **Edibility:** Considered inedible to mildly poisonous; reports of gastrointestinal symptoms exist. Should not be eaten.
- **Interesting fact:** Unlike its famous psychoactive cousins (*Psilocybe semilanceata*, *Psilocybe cubensis*), *S. coronilla* contains no psilocybin — but its grassland habitat and round-headed look frequently cause it to be misidentified by foragers.

## Parasol Mushroom (Macrolepiota procera)

- **Scientific name:** *Macrolepiota procera*
- **Habitat:** Found in pastures, meadow edges, woodland clearings, and roadsides on well-drained soils across Europe, parts of Asia, North America, and southern South America; fruits late summer through autumn.
- **Appearance:** Tall and elegant — stem 15–40 cm high, slender, hollow, with distinctive "snakeskin" zigzag brown patterning. Cap initially egg-shaped (drumstick-like), expanding to a wide parasol up to 30 cm across, pale buff with large shaggy brown scales and a darker central umbo. Bears a thick, double-edged movable ring on the stem. Gills white, free from the stem.
- **Edibility:** Excellent edible and highly prized when properly identified; cap is often breaded and pan-fried like a schnitzel. CAUTION: must be distinguished from toxic *Chlorophyllum* species (especially *C. molybdites*, with green spores) and small *Lepiota* species, some of which are deadly.
- **Interesting fact:** The movable ring can literally be slid up and down the stem — a useful field-identification character.

## Earth Tongue (Geoglossum spp.)

- **Scientific name:** *Geoglossum* spp. (e.g., *Geoglossum glabrum*, *G. cookeianum*)
- **Habitat:** Mossy unfertilized grasslands, lawns, dunes, and bogs across temperate regions of Europe, North America, and Asia. Often found alongside waxcaps in ancient grassland ecosystems.
- **Appearance:** Small, ground-emerging, club- or tongue-shaped fruiting body, 2–8 cm tall, entirely matte black or very dark brown. The fertile head is flattened and slightly grooved; the stem is finely scaly or smooth depending on species. Gives the impression of a tiny dark spike pushing up through moss.
- **Edibility:** Inedible — too small, tough, and of unknown safety; not consumed.
- **Interesting fact:** Earth tongues are ascomycetes (sac fungi), not basidiomycetes like most familiar mushrooms — their spores form inside microscopic asci on the club's surface. Like waxcaps, they are sensitive bioindicators of long-undisturbed, low-nutrient grasslands.
