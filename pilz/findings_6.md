# Findings — Batch 6 (Mushrooms 51–60)

## Artist's Conk (Ganoderma applanatum)

- **Scientific name:** *Ganoderma applanatum*
- **Habitat:** A widespread polypore on hardwood trees (especially beech, oak, maple, poplar) across temperate forests worldwide. Grows as a perennial bracket on living trees, dead snags, and fallen logs, causing white rot.
- **Appearance / ID:** Large, hard, hoof- or shelf-shaped fruiting body, often 10–60 cm wide, with a concentrically zoned grayish-brown to woody upper surface. The pure white pore surface bruises instantly dark brown when scratched — the signature feature.
- **Edibility:** Inedible due to its woody texture, but used medicinally; long history in traditional Asian medicine, often prepared as a tea or extract for immune-modulating polysaccharides.
- **Interesting fact:** Its name comes from the bruisable pore surface, which artists use as a natural canvas — drawings etched into a fresh specimen darken permanently as the conk dries, preserving the image for decades.

## Ink Cap (Coprinopsis atramentaria)

- **Scientific name:** *Coprinopsis atramentaria* (formerly *Coprinus atramentarius*), the common or tippler's ink cap.
- **Habitat:** Grows in clusters on or near buried wood, in lawns, parks, woodland edges, and disturbed soils across Europe, North America, and Asia, typically spring through autumn.
- **Appearance / ID:** Egg- to bell-shaped grayish-brown cap, 3–7 cm tall, with fine longitudinal grooves and pale fibrous scales near the apex. Gills start white, turn pink, then black, and the cap auto-digests (deliquesces) into a black inky liquid as it matures.
- **Edibility:** Edible when cooked, BUT toxic when consumed with alcohol — contains coprine, which inhibits acetaldehyde dehydrogenase and produces a severe disulfiram-like reaction (flushing, nausea, palpitations) for up to 72 hours after eating.
- **Interesting fact:** Its inky black liquid was historically collected and used as a writing ink; spores embedded in the ink can still be seen under a microscope on old documents.

## Fluted Bird's Nest (Cyathus striatus)

- **Scientific name:** *Cyathus striatus*
- **Habitat:** Saprotroph on decaying wood, twigs, mulch, and woody debris; common in gardens, forests, and landscaped beds throughout temperate Northern Hemisphere regions.
- **Appearance / ID:** Tiny (5–10 mm wide) cup- or vase-shaped fruiting bodies resembling miniature bird's nests, with shaggy brown exteriors and distinctively fluted/grooved (striate) interior walls. Inside sit several lens-shaped "eggs" (peridioles) containing the spores.
- **Edibility:** Inedible — far too small and tough to be of any culinary value, but not toxic.
- **Interesting fact:** It uses a splash-cup dispersal mechanism: a falling raindrop hits the cup at just the right angle to launch the peridioles up to a meter away, where a sticky thread (the funicular cord) tethers them to nearby vegetation.

## Black Trumpet (Craterellus cornucopioides)

- **Scientific name:** *Craterellus cornucopioides* (also *Craterellus fallax* in N. America)
- **Habitat:** Grows in mossy ground and leaf litter under hardwoods, especially oak and beech, often in shaded, damp depressions; widespread across Europe, North America, and Asia in late summer through fall.
- **Appearance / ID:** Hollow, trumpet- or horn-shaped fruiting body, 3–10 cm tall, dark gray to brown-black, with a wavy flaring rim. The outer (spore-bearing) surface is smooth to faintly wrinkled — there are no true gills. Resembles a small instrument or "horn of plenty."
- **Edibility:** Choice edible — highly prized in gourmet cooking for its rich, smoky, almost truffle-like flavor; dries and rehydrates exceptionally well.
- **Interesting fact:** Its dark color is excellent camouflage on the forest floor — foragers often describe it as "looking like a hole in the ground," and once you spot one you typically find dozens nearby.

## Bell Morel (Verpa bohemica)

- **Scientific name:** *Verpa bohemica* (early/wrinkled bell morel); *Verpa conica* is a closely related smooth-capped relative.
- **Habitat:** Found in early spring (often before true morels appear) in moist woodlands, river floodplains, and under cottonwoods, aspens, and elms across temperate North America and Europe.
- **Appearance / ID:** Thimble- or bell-shaped wrinkled brown cap, 2–5 cm tall, hanging skirt-like and attached only at the very top of a long, pale, hollow stem. Cut lengthwise it shows a free-hanging cap — a key distinction from true morels, whose cap is fused to the stem.
- **Edibility:** Edible with caution — eaten by some, but causes gastrointestinal upset and loss of muscle coordination in sensitive individuals, especially after multiple meals; many guides recommend avoiding it.
- **Interesting fact:** Bell morels are sometimes called "false morels" by foragers and are a frequent source of misidentification — the free-hanging cap is the single most reliable separator from the prized true *Morchella* species.

## Heavy Metal Fungi (hyperaccumulators)

- **Scientific names:** Includes *Agaricus* spp. (e.g., *A. arvensis*, *A. bisporus*), *Suillus luteus*, *Boletus edulis*, *Macrolepiota procera*, and others known to bioaccumulate cadmium, mercury, lead, copper, zinc, or radiocesium.
- **Habitat:** Worldwide; often most concerning along roadsides, near smelters, in industrial soils, and in regions with Chernobyl/Fukushima fallout where radiocesium persists in forest soils.
- **Appearance / ID:** No visual cue — heavy metal accumulation is a physiological trait of certain genera, not a morphological feature. Identification depends on knowing which species are accumulators.
- **Edibility:** Variable — many are otherwise choice edibles (porcini, parasol, meadow mushroom), but should be eaten in moderation when foraged from contaminated areas; metal levels in fruiting bodies can be 10–100x soil concentrations.
- **Interesting fact:** Mycoremediation exploits this trait — *Agaricus* and *Pleurotus* species are being studied to extract cadmium and lead from polluted soils, concentrating contaminants into easily harvested fruiting bodies.

## Porcini (Boletus edulis)

- **Scientific name:** *Boletus edulis* (the king bolete, cep, Steinpilz, penny bun)
- **Habitat:** Mycorrhizal partner of conifers (spruce, pine, fir) and hardwoods (oak, beech, birch); widespread across the Northern Hemisphere, fruiting summer to autumn.
- **Appearance / ID:** Large, robust mushroom with a smooth, glossy, brown bun-shaped cap (8–25 cm), thick bulbous white stem with a fine raised white reticulation (net pattern) on the upper portion, and a white-to-yellow-olive sponge-like pore surface (no gills) that does not bruise blue.
- **Edibility:** Choice edible — one of the world's most prized wild mushrooms, with a deep nutty, savory flavor; widely dried and traded internationally.
- **Interesting fact:** *Boletus edulis* is genuinely cosmopolitan — DNA studies show that populations from Europe, North America, and Asia are all the same species, an unusual situation among ectomycorrhizal fungi, which usually show strong geographic differentiation.

## Punkwood Fungus (Fomitopsis and allies)

- **Scientific names:** "Punkwood" refers to soft, dry, decayed wood produced by various wood-rotting fungi, notably *Fomitopsis pinicola* (red-belt conk), *Fomitopsis betulina* (birch polypore), *Fomes fomentarius* (tinder fungus), and *Laetiporus* spp.
- **Habitat:** On dead and dying conifers and hardwoods worldwide; the punky wood is found inside standing snags and fallen logs that have been colonized for years.
- **Appearance / ID:** The fungi are tough, woody bracket polypores; the "punkwood" itself is the lightweight, fibrous, often reddish-brown to buff degraded wood produced by their brown-rot enzymes, which break down cellulose while leaving lignin.
- **Edibility:** Inedible (woody) — but punkwood and the fungi themselves have major utilitarian value rather than culinary use.
- **Interesting fact:** Punkwood holds a coal extraordinarily well — Indigenous peoples and bushcrafters have used it for millennia as a fire-carrying medium, smoldering for hours and allowing fire to be transported between camps.

## Echo Fungus (hollow-log saprotrophs)

- **Scientific names:** Interpreted as fungi colonizing hollow logs and resonant cavities — typical inhabitants include *Pleurotus ostreatus* (oyster mushroom), *Armillaria* spp. (honey fungus), *Pholiota* spp., and *Hericium* species.
- **Habitat:** Inside and on hollowed-out logs, standing snags, and stumps in deciduous and mixed forests; many are heart-rot fungi that helped create the cavity in the first place.
- **Appearance / ID:** Highly variable depending on species — *Pleurotus* shows shelving fan-shaped caps with decurrent gills; *Armillaria* forms clustered honey-colored caps with a ring; the unifying ecological feature is fruiting from cracks, knotholes, or open ends of hollow trunks.
- **Edibility:** Variable — *Pleurotus ostreatus* is choice edible; *Armillaria* is edible only when well-cooked and causes upset in some; many other cavity dwellers (e.g., certain *Pholiota*) are inedible or mildly toxic.
- **Interesting fact:** The hollow logs these fungi create are critical wildlife habitat — by selectively decomposing heartwood while the tree still lives, heart-rot fungi engineer the cavities that owls, woodpeckers, bats, and bears depend on for nesting and denning.

## Lion's Mane (Hericium erinaceus)

- **Scientific name:** *Hericium erinaceus* (also called bearded tooth, monkey head, yamabushitake)
- **Habitat:** Grows on dead and dying hardwoods, especially American beech and oak, often emerging from wounds high on standing trunks; found across the Northern Hemisphere in late summer and autumn.
- **Appearance / ID:** Unmistakable — a single rounded, white to cream-colored mass, 5–40 cm wide, covered in long (1–4 cm) downward-hanging spines that produce the spores. No cap, no stem, no gills, just a shaggy "mane."
- **Edibility:** Choice edible and prized medicinal — texture and flavor often compared to lobster or crab meat when sautéed; widely cultivated.
- **Interesting fact:** Lion's mane contains hericenones and erinacines, compounds shown in laboratory and clinical studies to stimulate nerve growth factor (NGF) synthesis, generating ongoing research interest in its potential for cognitive support and peripheral nerve regeneration.
