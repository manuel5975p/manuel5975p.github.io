# Findings — Batch 9 (Mushrooms 81–90)

## Prototaxites

- **Scientific name:** *Prototaxites* (genus, several species including *P. logani*, *P. taiti*)
- **Habitat:** Extinct; lived during the Late Silurian to Late Devonian periods (approx. 430–360 million years ago) in terrestrial environments worldwide, when land plants were still small and primitive.
- **Distinctive appearance:** Massive trunk-like organisms, some specimens reaching up to 8 meters tall and 1 meter wide, making them the largest known terrestrial organisms of their time. Internal structure consists of interwoven tubes of varying diameters, resembling fungal hyphae.
- **Edibility:** Extinct — not applicable.
- **Interesting fact:** First described in 1859 and initially mistaken for a conifer (hence the "-taxites" suffix). Carbon isotope analysis published in 2007 strongly suggested it was a giant fungus, though some researchers continue to debate whether it might be a giant lichen or an entirely unique group with no modern analog.

## Ice Fungus (Hair Ice)

- **Scientific name:** *Exidiopsis effusa*
- **Habitat:** Grows on moist, rotting hardwood branches (especially beech) in temperate broadleaf forests, primarily between 45° and 55° latitude in the Northern Hemisphere. Active in humid winter conditions just below 0 °C.
- **Distinctive appearance:** The fungus itself is an inconspicuous grey-brown crust, but it is famous for producing "hair ice" — delicate, silky-white filaments of ice that grow from rotting wood, resembling fine cotton candy or wig hair. Each strand can be up to 20 cm long but only ~0.02 mm thick.
- **Edibility:** Inedible — not consumed; the visible "fruit" is ice, not fungal tissue.
- **Interesting fact:** The fungus shapes the ice crystals via a recrystallization inhibitor (lignin- and tannin-related compounds) that prevents the fine hairs from melting into bulk ice, allowing them to retain their hair-like form for hours.

## Renaissance Truffle (White Truffle)

- **Scientific name:** *Tuber magnatum* (Italian/Piedmont white truffle)
- **Habitat:** Grows underground in ectomycorrhizal symbiosis with the roots of oak, hazel, poplar, and beech trees. Found mainly in the Piedmont region of Italy (notably Alba), Istria (Croatia), and parts of the Balkans, in calcareous, well-drained soils.
- **Distinctive appearance:** Irregular, knobby tuber 3–12 cm in diameter with a smooth, pale yellow-ochre to cream-colored exterior. The interior (gleba) is marbled white to brown with fine white veining. Possesses an extraordinarily pungent, garlicky-musky aroma.
- **Edibility:** Edible — one of the most highly prized culinary delicacies in the world, eaten raw and shaved over dishes.
- **Interesting fact:** Prized since Renaissance Italy in noble courts, *T. magnatum* still cannot be reliably cultivated. It regularly auctions for €3,000–€6,000 per kilogram, with exceptional specimens fetching hundreds of thousands of euros at the annual Alba truffle auctions.

## Veiled Lady

- **Scientific name:** *Phallus indusiatus* (also *Dictyophora indusiata*)
- **Habitat:** Grows in tropical and subtropical regions on woody debris and rich humus in forests and gardens. Native to South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa, the Americas, and Australia.
- **Distinctive appearance:** A striking stinkhorn with a white, hollow stalk topped by a dark olive-greenish, slime-coated, conical cap. Its hallmark is a delicate, lacy white "skirt" (the indusium) hanging down from beneath the cap, reaching nearly to the ground — resembling fine lace netting.
- **Edibility:** Edible — the unexpanded "egg" stage and the mature stalk (after washing off slime) are considered a delicacy in Chinese cuisine, known as "zhú sūn" (bamboo fungus).
- **Interesting fact:** It is one of the fastest-growing organisms in the fungal kingdom, with the fruit body extending its lacy skirt fully in just 1–2 hours after emerging from its egg, making it a popular subject for time-lapse photography.

## Midnight Blue Entoloma

- **Scientific name:** *Entoloma hochstetteri*
- **Habitat:** Found in damp, mossy native forests of New Zealand (and reportedly in parts of India). Grows in leaf litter, often near *Nothofagus* (southern beech) and podocarp trees.
- **Distinctive appearance:** A small, slender mushroom with an entirely vivid indigo-blue to deep sky-blue coloration across cap, stem, and gills. The conical-to-bell-shaped cap (2–4 cm) has a small umbo, and the gills mature to a pinkish hue from spore deposit. Stem is fibrous and hollow.
- **Edibility:** Inedible / suspected toxic — many *Entoloma* species are poisonous, and *E. hochstetteri* is not consumed; toxicity status is uncertain.
- **Interesting fact:** This mushroom is depicted on the reverse of New Zealand's $50 banknote alongside a hihi (stitchbird), making it one of the very few fungi in the world featured on a national currency.

## Rosy Veined Entoloma

- **Scientific name:** *Entoloma rhodopolium* (closest match for a pink/rose-veined Entoloma)
- **Habitat:** Grows in deciduous and mixed woodlands, particularly under beech and oak, on rich humus soils throughout Europe, North America, and parts of East Asia. Fruits in late summer to autumn.
- **Distinctive appearance:** Cap 4–10 cm, initially convex then flattening, grey-brown to pale ochre with a silky-fibrous surface and a slight umbo; hygrophanous (color changes with moisture). Gills are characteristically white at first, becoming pink to rosy-flesh-colored as spores mature, giving the "rosy-veined" appearance. Stem white, fibrous, hollow.
- **Edibility:** Toxic — causes severe gastrointestinal poisoning (vomiting, diarrhea, cramps). Easily confused with edible *Clitopilus prunulus* or young *Calocybe gambosa*.
- **Interesting fact:** It is one of the most frequent causes of mushroom poisoning in Europe due to its resemblance to several edible species; the genus name *Entoloma* itself means "inward-turned margin," referring to the cap's incurved edge in young specimens.

## Dusky Puffball

- **Scientific name:** *Lycoperdon nigrescens* (synonym *Lycoperdon foetidum*); related to *Lycoperdon molle*
- **Habitat:** Grows on acidic soils in coniferous and mixed woodlands, heaths, and mossy areas across Europe and North America, typically appearing from late summer through autumn.
- **Distinctive appearance:** Pear- to top-shaped fruit body 2–5 cm tall, initially pale buff but quickly developing a covering of dark brown to nearly black, pointed, pyramidal spines that contrast against the lighter underlying surface. As it matures, spines fall away leaving a netted pattern, and an apical pore opens to release dark olive-brown spores.
- **Edibility:** Edible when very young (white interior), but generally not recommended due to a faintly unpleasant odor and easy confusion with similar species; mature specimens are inedible.
- **Interesting fact:** Like other puffballs, it disperses spores via raindrop impact — each falling drop creates a puff of spores ejected through the apical pore in a mechanism known as "ballistic ejection" or bellows dispersal.

## Yellow Morel

- **Scientific name:** *Morchella esculenta* (the "common" or "yellow" morel; actually a species complex)
- **Habitat:** Grows in temperate forests across the Northern Hemisphere, often near ash, elm, apple, and tulip poplar trees, as well as in disturbed ground and old orchards. Fruits in spring (March–May).
- **Distinctive appearance:** Distinctive honeycomb-like cap with irregular pits and ridges, ranging from pale yellow to tan or grey-brown. The cap is fully attached to the hollow stem at its base. Cap and stem are entirely hollow when sliced lengthwise — a key identification feature.
- **Edibility:** Edible — highly prized choice edible, but **must be cooked**; raw morels contain hydrazine toxins that cause gastrointestinal upset.
- **Interesting fact:** Morels are nearly impossible to commercially cultivate at scale (though some success has been achieved in China); they are confused with the toxic "false morels" (*Gyromitra* spp.), which contain the carcinogenic monomethylhydrazine and have a brain-like rather than honeycombed cap.

## Pumpkin Mushroom (Jack-O-Lantern)

- **Scientific name:** *Omphalotus olearius* (Europe) / *Omphalotus illudens* (eastern North America)
- **Habitat:** Grows in dense clusters at the base of hardwood trees and stumps (especially oak), or above buried roots, in temperate deciduous forests. Fruits in late summer through autumn.
- **Distinctive appearance:** Bright pumpkin-orange to saffron-yellow throughout, with a smooth, funnel-shaped cap 5–20 cm wide, and true (non-forked, sharp-edged) decurrent gills running down the stem. Grows in large fused clumps. Often confused with edible chanterelles, which have blunt false gills (ridges) and grow singly on the ground.
- **Edibility:** Toxic — contains illudin S and related sesquiterpenes that cause severe vomiting, diarrhea, and cramps (rarely fatal but extremely unpleasant).
- **Interesting fact:** Fresh gills emit a faint greenish bioluminescent glow ("foxfire") in the dark due to enzymatic luciferin–luciferase reactions, which is the source of its "Jack-O-Lantern" common name. The illudin compounds are also being studied as anticancer agents (the derivative irofulven has reached clinical trials).

## Velvet Foot (Enoki)

- **Scientific name:** *Flammulina velutipes*
- **Habitat:** Grows on dead or dying hardwoods (especially elm, willow, poplar, and ash) in temperate regions throughout the Northern Hemisphere. Notably one of the few mushrooms that fruits in late autumn and winter, sometimes pushing through snow.
- **Distinctive appearance:** In the wild: small (2–7 cm) cap, sticky orange-brown to tawny, with cream/white gills and a distinctive dark-brown to nearly black, velvety stem (hence "velvet foot"). The cultivated "enoki" form is dramatically different — long, slender, white, and tightly clustered, grown in CO₂-rich darkness to inhibit cap expansion and pigment.
- **Edibility:** Edible (cooked) — both wild and cultivated forms are choice culinary mushrooms; raw consumption is discouraged.
- **Interesting fact:** *F. velutipes* was one of the first mushrooms cultivated in space, carried aboard a Soviet space station to test fungal growth in microgravity. Outbreaks of listeriosis from contaminated commercial enoki have led to recalls in recent years, making cooking strongly recommended.
