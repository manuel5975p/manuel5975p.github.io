# Findings — Batch 1 (Mushrooms 1–10)

## Bleeding Tooth Fungus (Hydnellum peckii)

- **Scientific name:** *Hydnellum peckii*
- **Habitat:** Coniferous forests across North America, Europe, and parts of Asia (Korea, Iran). Forms mycorrhizal associations with conifers, especially pines and spruces.
- **Appearance:** Young specimens are striking — a velvety white to pinkish cap exudes a thick, blood-red liquid through tiny pores via guttation, giving it the nicknames "strawberries and cream" and "Devil's tooth." The underside has small tooth-like spines instead of gills. Older specimens turn brown and lose their dramatic red droplets.
- **Edibility:** Inedible — not toxic but extremely bitter and peppery, making it unpalatable.
- **Interesting fact:** The red pigment contains atromentin, a compound with anticoagulant properties similar to heparin, and the species is being studied for potential pharmaceutical use.

## Destroying Angel (Amanita)

- **Scientific name:** *Amanita bisporigera* (eastern North America), *Amanita virosa* (Europe), *Amanita ocreata* (western North America).
- **Habitat:** Mixed and deciduous woodlands, often associated with oak, birch, and pine. Mycorrhizal.
- **Appearance:** Pure white throughout — cap, gills, stem, and spore print. Smooth white cap (5–15 cm), free white gills, white ring (annulus) on the stem, and a prominent white sac-like volva at the base. Resembles edible button mushrooms or puffballs when young.
- **Edibility:** Deadly. Among the most poisonous mushrooms known.
- **Interesting fact:** Contains amatoxins that inhibit RNA polymerase II, causing irreversible liver and kidney failure. Symptoms typically appear 6–24 hours after ingestion — long after toxins have done critical damage. A single cap can kill an adult.

## Black Truffle (Tuber melanosporum)

- **Scientific name:** *Tuber melanosporum*
- **Habitat:** Underground (hypogeous), in calcareous soils in symbiosis with the roots of oak, hazel, and hornbeam trees. Native to southern Europe — particularly France (Périgord), Italy, and Spain.
- **Appearance:** Irregularly rounded, walnut- to fist-sized blackish "tuber" with a rough, pyramidal-warty exterior. The interior flesh (gleba) is dark brown to black with fine white veins (marbling). Intensely aromatic.
- **Edibility:** Edible and prized as a luxury delicacy.
- **Interesting fact:** Truffles cannot be cultivated easily and rely on trained dogs (or historically pigs) to detect their scent underground. Fresh black truffles can sell for €1,000–€2,500 per kilogram, and their aroma compounds (especially dimethyl sulfide) attract animals that disperse the spores.

## Golden Chanterelle (Cantharellus cibarius)

- **Scientific name:** *Cantharellus cibarius*
- **Habitat:** Coniferous and deciduous forests across the Northern Hemisphere, in mossy ground, mycorrhizal with oak, beech, birch, and pine.
- **Appearance:** Egg-yolk to golden yellow throughout. Funnel- or vase-shaped cap with a wavy, irregular margin. Crucially, instead of true gills it has shallow, forked, blunt ridges (false gills) running down the stem. Firm, pale yellow flesh with a fruity, apricot-like aroma.
- **Edibility:** Edible and highly prized.
- **Interesting fact:** Beware the toxic look-alike *Omphalotus olearius* (Jack-o'-lantern), which has true sharp gills and is bioluminescent. The chanterelle's apricot scent is a key distinguishing feature, and it contains high levels of vitamin D when exposed to sunlight.

## Indigo Milk Cap (Lactarius indigo)

- **Scientific name:** *Lactarius indigo*
- **Habitat:** Deciduous and coniferous forests in eastern North America, Central America, and East Asia. Mycorrhizal with oaks and pines.
- **Appearance:** Stunning indigo blue throughout when fresh, with concentric darker zones on the cap. The cap (5–15 cm) is initially convex then becomes funnel-shaped. When cut or broken, it bleeds a dark blue "milk" (latex) that slowly turns greenish on exposure to air. Older specimens fade to silvery- or grayish-blue.
- **Edibility:** Edible, though texture is somewhat grainy. Sold in markets in Mexico and Guatemala.
- **Interesting fact:** Its blue color comes from a unique azulene derivative (7-isopropenyl-4-methylazulene-1-carboxylic acid) — the only known natural occurrence of such a pigment in fungi.

## Amethyst Deceiver (Laccaria amethystina)

- **Scientific name:** *Laccaria amethystina*
- **Habitat:** Deciduous and coniferous woodlands across Europe, North America, and Asia. Mycorrhizal, especially with beech and oak.
- **Appearance:** Small (1–6 cm cap) with an entirely deep violet-purple color when fresh and moist — cap, widely spaced gills, and fibrous stem. The "deceiver" name comes from its tendency to fade dramatically when dry, becoming buff or grayish, making it surprisingly hard to identify.
- **Edibility:** Edible, but caps only — flavor is mild and undistinguished.
- **Interesting fact:** It is a known hyperaccumulator of arsenic, concentrating the element from the soil into its fruiting bodies at levels far above background, so foragers are advised against eating large amounts, especially from contaminated or industrial areas.

## Pink Oyster Mushroom (Pleurotus djamor)

- **Scientific name:** *Pleurotus djamor*
- **Habitat:** Tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, growing as a saprotroph on dead and dying hardwoods. Native range includes Southeast Asia, Australia, and the Americas.
- **Appearance:** Vivid salmon-pink to bright rose-pink fan- or shelf-shaped clusters with crowded gills running down a very short or absent stem. The bright pink color fades to pale tan or yellowish with age. Tender, thin flesh.
- **Edibility:** Edible — best young, when the texture is still tender; older fruiting bodies become tough.
- **Interesting fact:** It is one of the fastest-growing cultivated mushrooms, capable of producing fruiting bodies in 3–4 weeks from inoculation, and it has notable mycoremediation potential — degrading petroleum hydrocarbons and even some plastics.

## Parrot Waxcap (Gliophorus psittacinus)

- **Scientific name:** *Gliophorus psittacinus* (formerly *Hygrocybe psittacina*)
- **Habitat:** Unimproved, nutrient-poor grasslands, lawns, and mossy meadows across Europe, North America, and Australasia. A grassland indicator species.
- **Appearance:** Small (1–4 cm cap) and slimy, starting bright parrot-green and gradually shifting through yellow, orange, and pink as it matures, often displaying multiple colors on a single cap. The whole fruit body is coated in thick, gelatinous slime.
- **Edibility:** Inedible — too small and slimy to be of culinary value, though not toxic.
- **Interesting fact:** Waxcaps are indicators of ancient, unfertilized grasslands; their presence often signals high-quality, undisturbed habitat, and many waxcap species are now red-listed in Europe due to agricultural intensification.

## Sky Blue Mushroom (Entoloma hochstetteri)

- **Scientific name:** *Entoloma hochstetteri*
- **Habitat:** Native to New Zealand and parts of India; grows on the forest floor in damp native bush, typically among leaf litter under southern beech and podocarp trees.
- **Appearance:** A vivid, almost unreal indigo to sky-blue color throughout — conical to bell-shaped cap (2–4 cm) with a small pointed umbo, slender blue stem, and bluish gills that mature to salmon-pink as pinkish spores develop. The color comes from three azulene pigments.
- **Edibility:** Unknown / suspected toxic. Many *Entoloma* species are poisonous, so it is not eaten.
- **Interesting fact:** It is featured on the reverse of the New Zealand $50 banknote — one of the very few fungi to appear on national currency — and is considered a taonga (treasured) species in Maori culture.

## Chicken of the Woods (Laetiporus sulphureus)

- **Scientific name:** *Laetiporus sulphureus*
- **Habitat:** A parasitic and saprotrophic bracket fungus on living and dead hardwoods, especially oak, but also chestnut, cherry, and willow. Common throughout temperate Europe and North America.
- **Appearance:** Large, dramatic overlapping shelves or rosettes (10–50 cm or more across) in vivid sulfur-yellow to bright orange on top, with a brilliant yellow pore surface beneath (no gills). Soft and suede-like when young, becoming chalky and brittle with age.
- **Edibility:** Edible when young and fresh — the meaty texture and mild flavor genuinely resemble chicken. Some people experience gastrointestinal upset, especially from specimens grown on conifers, eucalyptus, or yew.
- **Interesting fact:** It causes brown cubical rot in living trees, breaking down cellulose and leaving lignin, and a single tree can produce many kilograms of edible fruit body year after year from the same mycelium.
